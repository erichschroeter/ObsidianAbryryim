**Owen Blackwood**

At twenty-two, Owen Blackwood already carries himself like a man who has
lived through two lifetimes. He is broad-shouldered, steady-eyed, and a
little too young to look as tired as he sometimes does. His face is open
enough that people tend to trust him, but there is always something
guarded behind the expression, as though part of him is still listening
for shouted orders, marching boots, or the crack of a distant bowstring.
He is not grim by nature. In fact, when he speaks, it is often with a
dry wit and a crooked half-smile that suggests he has learned the only
way to survive hard things is to laugh at them before they swallow you
whole.

Owen was born far from Pistivo, in a small timber settlement where the
winters were lean and everyone learned early that survival depended less
on glory and more on endurance. His family were good people, not
wealthy, not important, but dependable. His father taught him how to
split wood, mend a fence, and keep his word. His mother taught him that
kindness and softness were not the same thing, and that decent people
sometimes had to stand in ugly places because no one else would. Owen
grew up on stories of honorable knights and victorious generals, but
real life offered fewer banners and more hunger. When his village fell
on hard years and the local lord's recruiters came promising coin,
meals, and purpose, Owen enlisted before he was truly old enough to
understand what service would cost him.

He had expected discipline, brotherhood, maybe even a little glory. Some
of that was real. The army taught him how to wear armor until it felt
like skin, how to hold a shield line when every instinct screamed to
run, how to read the mood of a camp by the silence between voices. He
learned the kind of loyalty forged only by shared misery. He learned
that some men become family simply because they stood beside you when
the world was trying to kill you. But he also learned that wars are
rarely about the reasons printed on banners. Officers made speeches
about honor while farmers buried sons in muddy fields. Orders came down
from men who slept warm and far from arrows. Villages burned because
someone in command decided the cost was acceptable. Owen did what he was
told longer than he likes to admit, because soldiers tell themselves
obedience is survival. It is easier to carry out an order than to ask
whether it deserves to be obeyed.

The moment that broke him was not some great heroic last stand. It was
smaller than that, and because it was smaller, it has stayed with him.
His unit was sent to seize supplies from a settlement accused of aiding
rebels, deserters, or bandits depending on which officer was telling the
tale. They found frightened families, not fighters. Hungry people. A few
hidden weapons, maybe, but nothing that justified what followed. The
command was to make an example of the place. Owen remembers the
torchlight, the crying, the hard faces of men pretending not to hear
either. He remembers disobeying one order and following another, trying
to save who he could while still wearing the colors of the ones causing
the harm. He got several people out. Not enough. Never enough.

After that, he stopped believing that rank and righteousness were
cousins. He finished out what he could, took a beating for
insubordination, and left the service with his body intact but his faith
in causes badly wounded. He did not desert in the technical sense, but
neither did he part on noble terms. To some old commanders, Owen
Blackwood is a disappointing soldier who never learned his place. To the
common folk who met him on the road after, he is often something
simpler: a man with a sword who still remembers what swords are supposed
to be for.

In the months since leaving the army, Owen has drifted from contract to
contract and road to road, taking honest work where he can. Guard duty.
Caravan escort. Bouncer work in rough taverns. The occasional ugly job
that becomes less ugly because he refuses to let it go too far. He is
not a mercenary in spirit, though coin matters more than pride when your
boots are worn thin. He tells himself he is only passing through places,
only surviving, only minding his own business. Yet he has a habit of
stepping in when someone weaker is being cornered, cheated, or
threatened. He complains the whole time, too, like a man deeply annoyed
by his own conscience, but he steps in anyway. That is Owen in truth:
practical, skeptical, and wary of grand ideals, yet unable to walk away
when people need help.

His alignment, if anyone bothered to pin such things to a board, would
sit squarely at **neutral good**. Owen does not trust systems, titles,
uniforms, or polished speeches. He trusts people, slowly and case by
case. Rules matter only as much as the good they protect. If breaking
one saves a life, he will break it and sleep just fine. If keeping his
word puts him in danger, he will likely curse under his breath and keep
it anyway. He is not driven by glory, holiness, revenge, or ambition. He
is driven by a simpler creed he rarely says aloud: people should not be
left to suffer when you have the strength to help.

That is part of why he came to **Pistivo** for the funeral of **Devlin
Quicksilver**.

Owen did not know Devlin as the quiet silver-smith the town knew. He
knew him first as an older traveler with patient eyes and hands that
looked as comfortable around tools as they did around trouble. They met
on the road a year or two ago, when Owen was fresh out of the army and
still carrying all the sharp edges that come from trying to decide what
sort of man you are after the world has made use of you. Owen had taken
work guarding a merchant wagon and nearly gotten himself killed by a
roadside horror he could barely name. Devlin had been there by chance,
or perhaps by the sort of chance that follows old adventurers around. He
did not save Owen cleanly or dramatically. He helped, certainly, but
mostly he steadied the moment. He showed Owen where to stand, when to
wait, when to strike. Afterward, while Owen expected a lecture, Devlin
simply shared a fire and told him that surviving long enough to become
useful was a skill worth respecting.

That stayed with Owen.

In the weeks that followed, their paths crossed twice more. Devlin never
spoke much of his past, but Owen could tell the stories in the man's
scars and silences. What Devlin offered was not mentorship in the formal
sense. It was something rarer: the example of a man who had seen
terrible things and had not let them sour him completely. Devlin did not
preach heroics. He spoke instead about craft, patience, and
responsibility. About how a blade is only noble in the hands of someone
who knows when not to draw it. About how surviving is not the same as
living. About how a man can spend years breaking things and still
choose, one day, to make something instead. To Owen, who had left
soldiering with more bitterness than direction, those lessons landed
hard.

So when word reached him that Devlin Quicksilver had died, Owen came to
Pistivo for the funeral for one reason alone: respect. Perhaps also
debt. Not the kind paid in coin, but the kind paid by showing up when it
matters. He will not make a speech unless forced. He will stand at the
back, hat in hand, shoulders stiff, and look like a man who would rather
face a charging beast than speak about what another man meant to him.
But he came. That says enough.

Now Pistivo is a city tied to memory for him. He arrived expecting only
to pay his respects and move on. Yet funerals have a way of gathering
the living around unfinished things. Owen can feel it already: the odd
weight in the air, the sense that Devlin's story did not end cleanly
with a grave and a prayer. The boots by the forge door. The silver that
seems to remember heat. The sort of rumors sensible men dismiss right
before they become involved in them. Owen tells himself he is not here
for adventure. He has had enough of causes and legends. But if Devlin
left behind a loose thread, a lingering danger, or a chance to protect
people in the old man's name, Owen knows exactly what will happen. He
will sigh, mutter something dry about bad decisions, tighten his grip on
his weapon, and step forward anyway.

Because for all his caution, for all his distrust, Owen Blackwood is
still the kind of man who answers the knock at the door.

**Personality notes for play**

**Temperament:** calm, observant, likable, dry humor, slow to trust
authority but quick to protect ordinary people.

**Strengths:** dependable under pressure, brave when it counts, good at
reading danger, naturally protective, practical leader in tense
situations.

**Flaws:** cynical about institutions, carries guilt from military
service, sometimes pushes people away before they can disappoint him,
acts like he has no ideals even when he clearly does.

**Moral center:** do the most good you can for the people in front of
you, even if the rules disagree.
